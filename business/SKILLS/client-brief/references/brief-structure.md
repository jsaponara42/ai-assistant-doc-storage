# Brief Structure Reference

This is the section-by-section template for a client brief. Follow this order and these labels exactly. Match the writing style of the examples: short, declarative, factual sentences. State names, numbers, roles, and dollar figures plainly. No filler, no hedging, no marketing tone.

Use markdown headings as shown. Use `> ⚠️ NEEDS INPUT: <question>` for anything the transcript doesn't supply. Label reasonable inferences with `*(inferred)*`.

---

## Title block

The brief opens with the client/company name as an H1, and a one-line descriptor of who they are.

If a primary internal contact is known, list their name, title, and contact details at the very top (phone, mobile, email) — these are the person you'll coordinate with.

---

## 1. Client and Organization

Plain facts about the entity:
- Legal/business name and any trade name (e.g. "operating online as X").
- Principal / CEO / owner: name, title, contact details.
- Headquarters address, main phone.
- Any other identifying basics the transcript gives.

---

## 2. Business Profile

A dense paragraph (or two) describing what the business actually does and the shape it's in:
- What they sell / their model / who they serve.
- How they make money and how disciplined each side of the operation is.
- Their growth stage and any seasonal or timing pressures.
- Distinct product or service lines — describe each one and note which is the growth engine.
- Where the engagement entered (referral, inbound, etc.) and current phase/mode.

This section establishes context for everything below. Capture nuance: which parts of the business are well-run vs. unmanaged.

---

## 3. Goals

Split into two:

**Personal Goals** — what the owner/principal wants for themselves (e.g. reclaim their time, step out of a role, reposition their authority, build something self-managing).

**Business Goals** — what the organization needs to achieve (e.g. shift the team's skill set, make a platform decision, become the dominant brand in their category).

These are the targets the whole plan serves. Pull them from what the principal says they want, explicitly or implicitly.

---

## 4. Key People — Internal

One entry per internal person. For each, capture:
- **Name, role/title.**
- **Working Style** — a plain description of how they operate and how to work with them effectively (e.g. "results-only, allergic to micromanagement, wants a right hand who keeps up without being managed step by step"). Describe the style in plain words. Do **not** use branded profile names.
- Tenure, what they own, and any watch-points (capacity limits, reliability concerns).
- Contact details if given.
- Their relevance to the engagement (ally, anchor, conduit, risk).

Be honest and specific about performance and fit — including underperformers — the same way the source brief is. This is an internal working document.

---

## 5. Key People — External (Vendors & Partners)

One entry per outside vendor, partner, or advisor. For each:
- **Name, company, role,** contact details if given.
- What they're proposing or delivering, including pricing/terms when stated (fees, monthly rates, scopes, timelines).
- How they relate to the engagement and to each other.
- Any decision that's pending on them (hold, evaluate, decline).

Capture the money precisely — dollar amounts, monthly fees, scoping costs.

---

## 6. Technology Stack

One entry per system/tool. For each:
- What it is and what it's used for.
- Its current state (heavily built but underused, static with no API, under evaluation, etc.).
- Integration gaps and pending decisions (replace, migrate, sunset).

Cover CRM, financial software, marketing/automation tools, AI tooling, and anything else operationally relevant.

---

## 7. Problem Register

The analytical core. Each diagnosed problem is its own subsection with an ID (`P-001`, `P-002`, …). For every problem capture these four parts plus area/status:

- **Area** — which part of the business it sits in (People, Processes, Systems, Tools, Resources).
- **Problem** — the dysfunction, stated concretely. What's actually happening or not happening, and the consequence.
- **Current Thinking** — the belief or assumption inside the business that keeps the problem in place ("the team knows the process, writing it down is overhead").
- **Reframe** — the corrected way of seeing it that the engagement will install ("what lives in people's heads leaves with them; documentation is infrastructure").
- **Approach** — the intervention tactic, and a status (Not started / In progress / Done).

Keep the contrast between Current Thinking and Reframe sharp — that pairing is the heart of each entry.

---

## 8. Action Plan

The roadmap that resolves the Problem Register. Organize by business area (People, Processes, Tools, Systems, Resources). Under each area, list the problems in scope and the **Workstreams** that address them.

For each Workstream:
- **Approach** — the framing/tactic for this piece of work.
- **What it involves** — the concrete steps.
- **Key Result** — the measurable outcome that means it's done.
- **Timeline** — weeks, and whether it runs concurrent with or after other workstreams.

Then provide a **Recommended Sequence** — a numbered ordering of all workstreams with dependencies noted (what must finish before what). Sequencing matters: foundation before tools, standards before platform decisions.

Open the Action Plan with a short **Plan Overview** paragraph: how many problems, where the constraint cluster is, what the principal's top stated priority actually maps to, and the core sequencing logic.

---

## 9. Engagement Metrics

A short baseline scorecard for tracking the engagement's health. Include the dimensions the source uses, relabeled plainly — for example:
- **Comprehension** — does the client understand the plan.
- **Adherence** — are they following it.
- **Adoption** — is it taking hold.
- **Velocity** — how fast the client is moving (and whether they're moving faster than the plan can support — a risk).
- **Volume** — how much is in flight.

At the first brief these are usually "Ungraded — baseline." Note any that are already a live watch-point.

---

## 10. Recommended Service Tier

A short recommendation: what level of ongoing support this engagement needs and why, with a price range if the user works from defined tiers. Justify it from the number of active problems, concurrency of workstreams, and coordination load across vendors.

---

## Changelog (updates only)

When extending an existing brief from a new meeting, add a dated entry at the very top of the document recording what changed: decisions made, people added or exited, vendors chosen, problems opened or closed.
