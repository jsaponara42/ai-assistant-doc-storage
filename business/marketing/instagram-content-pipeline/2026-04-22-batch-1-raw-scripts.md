## Meats

*Opens and closes clean. No hook dependency. No CTA segue. Written for a complete stranger.*

[Meat 1 — Lead Leakage | Source: Present | Retain: Story + Fact]

Most personal injury firms convert somewhere between five and ten percent of their inbound leads. Which means for every hundred people who reach out — ninety of them never become clients. That number surprised me the first time I really sat with it.

And when I dig into why, it's almost never the leads. The leads are fine. People are calling because they got hurt and they need a lawyer. The intent is real.

What's happening is the follow-up. It's slow. It's inconsistent. Sometimes it just doesn't happen.

There's research on this that I keep coming back to. If you contact a lead within the first minute of them reaching out, they're something like four times more likely to convert than if you wait an hour. Four times.

And most firms aren't calling in a minute. They're calling in hours — because someone has to see the form, decide it's a priority, find a gap between other things, and pick up the phone.

By the time that happens, that person has already talked to two or three other attorneys.

The leads aren't the problem. What happens right after the lead comes in — that's where the money is going.

---

[Meat 2 — The Hiring Myth | Source: Recent Past | Retain: List + Story]

I had a conversation recently with a PI firm owner who was about to hire their third intake coordinator in two years.

And I asked one question: what does this person spend most of their time on?

The answer was — chasing clients for documents, sending case status updates, copying information from one system into another.

And here's what I've noticed about that kind of work. It's not intake work. It's not legal work. It's not even administrative work that requires a person. It's the kind of work that exists because a system was never built to handle it.

Every hire like that adds a salary, a benefits package, onboarding time, management load — and it solves the symptom without touching the cause.

I'm not saying never hire. Sometimes you need people. But in a lot of the firms I talk to, the next hire is a workaround for a process that was never built correctly.

Fixing the process is a one-time thing. Hiring is a monthly cost that compounds.

It's worth asking the question before you post the job.

[Meat 3 — The Attorney as Bottleneck | Source: Present | Retain: Story + List]

In almost every PI firm I've worked with, the attorney ends up involved in things that don't need a law degree.

Approving small decisions. Answering client check-in calls. Chasing down a missing document. Reviewing something a paralegal could have handled.

And it's not because they want to be doing those things. It's because the systems around them were never built out, so everything flows through them by default.

Here's the problem with that.

You have someone who spent a decade getting licensed, who can negotiate a six-figure settlement, spending part of their day on tasks that could be handled automatically — or by someone earning a fraction of their rate.

And there's a ceiling. There's a hard limit on how many cases a firm can carry when the attorney is in the critical path of daily operations. You can't grow past it without removing yourself from those decisions first.

That's not a hiring problem. That's a systems problem.

---

[Meat 4 — The DIY Tools Trap | Source: Recent Past | Retain: Story + List]

Most PI firms I talk to have already tried the software route.

Clio. Lawmatics. Leaddocket. Good tools — genuinely. And most of the time, they didn't stick.

What I've found is that it almost never failed because the tool was wrong. It failed because setting up a tool correctly — actually mapping it to how the firm works, training the staff, maintaining it — takes time the attorney doesn't have.

So it gets half-built. The staff uses it inconsistently. It becomes another thing to manage on top of everything else. And eventually it gets written off.

"We tried that. It didn't work for us."

But what didn't work wasn't the tool. What didn't work was doing a complex implementation on top of an already-full workload with no one responsible for making it succeed.

The tool was never the issue. Ownership was.

[Meat 5 — Speed to Contact | Source: Present | Retain: Fact + Story]

There's a stat about PI lead conversion that I keep coming back to.

If you contact a lead within the first minute of them reaching out — one minute — they're about four times more likely to convert than if you wait five minutes. Not an hour. Five minutes.

Four times more likely.

And I think most PI attorneys hear that and think — okay, I need to be faster. But the problem isn't effort. Nobody is slow on purpose.

The problem is the system. Someone has to notice the form came in. Decide it's worth calling right now. Find a break between whatever else is happening. Pick up the phone.

That chain has friction in it. And in the time that friction takes — that person has already called two or three other firms.

Most people reaching out to a PI attorney after an accident are not waiting for the best lawyer. They're hiring the first one who makes them feel heard.

Speed isn't a nice-to-have. In this practice area, it's the competition.

[Meat 6 — The Hidden Cost of Manual Work | Source: Manufactured | Retain: Steps + Story]

Here's a quick exercise I walk firms through when we first start talking.

Pick one task your staff does manually every week. Something repetitive — sending a status update, chasing a document, entering information from one place into another.

Now estimate how many hours a week that takes across your whole team.

Now multiply that by your fully-loaded staff cost per hour — salary plus benefits, roughly.

Now multiply by fifty-two.

Most firms, when they do that math for the first time, get a number that surprises them. Not because the task felt expensive — it didn't. It felt like just part of the job.

But a task that takes ten hours a week at thirty dollars an hour fully loaded is fifteen thousand dollars a year. For one task.

And most firms have three, four, five of those running at the same time.

That's not the cost of automation. That's what the manual approach is already costing — quietly, every year, without ever showing up as a line item.

[Meat 7 — The Firm That Runs Without You | Source: Far Past | Retain: Story + Steps]

When I first started working inside businesses that had real systems — not just good people — I noticed something that felt almost strange.

Things happened without anyone being asked.

Not because the people were extraordinary. Because the process was clear enough that ordinary people could follow it without needing direction.

That's the version of a firm a lot of PI attorneys say they want. And when I ask what it looks like day to day, there's usually a pause — because the goal is clear but the path isn't.

What I've seen work is not one big overhaul. It's a series of small removals.

You stop being the person who approves that one thing. You build a follow-up sequence so you don't have to remember to call. You document how a case gets handed off so the next person doesn't need to ask you.

One thing at a time. Each one is boring. Together they add up to something that starts to run without you at the center of it.

Nobody gets there in a month. But the firms that get there at all started by removing themselves from one small thing — and then the next.

[Meat 8 — The Case That Slips | Source: Recent Past | Retain: Story]

I heard a version of this story recently from a PI attorney, and I've heard variations of it more times than I can count.

A case was moving along. No red flags. Good liability, decent damages. And then something small got missed — a follow-up call that didn't happen, a document that was never collected, a deadline that slipped past in a busy week.

It didn't kill the case. But it cost them. The settlement was lower than it should have been. Maybe it prolonged the timeline. Maybe the client's trust took a hit.

And the attorney wasn't careless. They were managing thirty other things at the same time.

In a contingency practice, that kind of slip doesn't just affect one case. It affects the revenue that case was supposed to generate. It affects the client's outcome. And it's almost always avoidable — not by working harder or being more careful, but by building a system where the small things don't depend on someone remembering them.

Memory is not a system. It's a risk.

[Meat 9 — "We Already Tried This" | Source: Recent Past | Retain: List + Story]

A lot of PI firm owners I talk to have already written off automation. And when I ask why, it almost always comes back to the same experience.

They tried a tool. It didn't work. And the conclusion they drew was that automation doesn't work for firms like theirs.

I understand that conclusion. But in most cases, it's wrong.

What I try to figure out is what actually happened. And after enough of these conversations, it's almost always one of three things.

The tool wasn't configured for how their firm actually works — it was set up generically and expected to fit.

Nobody owned the implementation. It got handed to a paralegal who was already at capacity, and it never got finished.

Or it got built once and never touched again — and as the firm evolved, the system stopped matching reality.

None of those are reasons automation doesn't work. They're reasons that specific attempt didn't work.

The conclusion was wrong. The history was real.

---

[Meat 10 — Why More Ad Spend Doesn't Fix It | Source: Present | Retain: Story + Fact]

There's a pattern I've noticed in PI firms that have hit a revenue plateau.

The instinct is always to spend more on leads. More ads, more SEO, more referral outreach. And the logic makes sense on the surface — more leads should mean more cases.

But if a firm is converting seven or eight percent of inbound leads, doubling the lead volume just means double the unconverted leads. The math doesn't change.

I've seen firms spend significantly more on advertising and barely move their revenue — because the intake process couldn't keep up with the volume it was already getting, let alone more of it.

Before you buy more traffic, it's worth understanding what's happening to the traffic you already have.

In a lot of cases I've looked at, improving what happens after a lead comes in produces more revenue than buying more leads — and it costs less.

More leads into a broken process is just a more expensive version of the same problem.
