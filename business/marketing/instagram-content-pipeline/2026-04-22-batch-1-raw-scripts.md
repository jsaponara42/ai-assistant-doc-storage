## Meats

*One idea per clip. Opens and closes clean. No hook dependency, no CTA.*

[Meat 1 — The Lead Leakage Problem]
Most PI firms convert somewhere between 5 and 10 percent of their leads. That means for every 100 people who reach out — 90 or more never become clients. And in my experience, it's rarely because the leads were bad. It's because the follow-up was slow, or inconsistent, or it just didn't happen at all. The research on this is pretty wild — the difference between calling a lead back in one minute versus one hour is enormous. By the time most firms get to someone, that person has already talked to someone else. The leads aren't the problem. What happens after the lead comes in — that's where the money is going.

[Meat 2 — The Hiring Myth]
When a firm gets busy, the instinct is always to hire. Get another paralegal. Bring on an intake coordinator. And sometimes that's the right call. But what I've noticed is that a lot of the hiring happens before anyone's asked whether the work actually needs a person. A lot of what paralegals spend time on — chasing clients for documents, sending status updates, moving information from one system to another — those aren't human problems. They're system problems. Hiring adds overhead and training time and management load. Fixing the system is a one-time thing. I'm not saying never hire. I'm saying it's worth asking the question first.

[Meat 3 — The Attorney as Bottleneck]
Something I see in almost every PI firm I talk to — the attorney is involved in things that don't require a law degree. Approving small decisions. Answering client check-in calls. Chasing down missing paperwork. And it's not because they want to be doing those things. It's because the systems around them never got built out. So everything flows through them by default. The problem with that is you've got someone billing at the top of the market spending time on tasks that could be handled at a fraction of the cost — or handled automatically. And there's a ceiling on how many cases a firm can take when the attorney is in the critical path of daily operations.

[Meat 4 — The DIY Tools Trap]
A lot of PI firms have tried the software route. Clio, Lawmatics, Leaddocket — good tools. And most of the time they didn't stick. Not because the tools were bad, but because setting them up correctly takes time the attorney doesn't have. So it gets half-implemented. Or the staff uses it inconsistently. Or it just becomes another thing to manage on top of everything else. What I've noticed is that the tool is never really the issue. The issue is who's going to own the implementation — and in a small firm, nobody has the bandwidth to do it right. That's where most of the "we tried automation and it didn't work" stories come from.

[Meat 5 — Speed to Contact]
There's a stat I keep coming back to when I talk to PI firms. If you contact a lead within the first minute of them reaching out, they're something like four times more likely to convert than if you wait even five minutes. Four times. And most firms aren't responding in a minute. They're responding in hours — if at all. Because someone has to see the form come in, decide it's a priority, find time between other things, and make the call. By the time that happens, that person has already called two or three other firms. Speed matters more in PI than almost any other practice area I've seen — because most people are calling multiple attorneys at the same time.

[Meat 6 — The Invisible Cost of Manual Work]
Something that doesn't show up on a P&L is the cost of manual work that could be automated. If you've got a paralegal spending ten hours a week on tasks a system could handle — that's ten hours of salary going toward something that doesn't require a human. Multiply that by fifty-two weeks. Most firms I talk to have never done that math. When you actually put a number on it, it changes the conversation. It's not about the cost of building a system. It's about what the manual approach is already costing — and has been costing, quietly, for years.

[Meat 7 — What "The Firm Running Without Me" Actually Looks Like]
A lot of PI attorneys tell me their goal is to build a firm that can run without them. And when I ask what that looks like day to day, there's usually a pause. Because the goal makes sense in theory, but the path from here to there isn't clear. In my experience, it's not one big change. It's a series of small ones — removing yourself from one decision, then another. Automating one follow-up sequence, then the next. It's boring work. But every thing you systematize is one less thing that needs you in it. The firms I've seen get there did it gradually, not all at once.

[Meat 8 — The Case That Falls Apart]
The scenario I hear more than any other from PI attorneys is some version of this: a case was going fine, something small got missed — a deadline, a document, a follow-up that didn't happen — and it cost them. Sometimes it costs them the case. Sometimes just a settlement that should've been higher. And it's never because anyone was careless. It's because they were managing thirty other things at the same time and something slipped. In a contingency-based practice, one missed detail can wipe out months of work. The solution isn't to work harder or be more careful. It's to build the kind of system where the small things don't depend on someone remembering them.

[Meat 9 — Why "We've Tried This Before" Doesn't Mean It Won't Work]
Almost every PI firm owner I talk to has some version of "we tried automation and it didn't work." And I never push back on that — because they're usually right. It didn't work. What I try to understand is why. And it's almost always one of three things: the tool wasn't set up right, nobody owned the implementation, or it was built for a generic business and not a PI firm specifically. That history doesn't mean automation doesn't work for them. It usually means the last attempt didn't have the right setup or the right support behind it. The outcome was real. The conclusion was wrong.

[Meat 10 — The Real Reason Marketing Spend Doesn't Scale]
Some PI firms pour money into ads and don't understand why the revenue doesn't grow at the same rate. The leads come in — and they do. But if the conversion rate stays at seven or eight percent, more leads just means more unconverted leads. The math doesn't change. I've seen firms where doubling the ad spend barely moved the needle on revenue — because the intake process couldn't capture the volume it was getting. Before you spend more on marketing, it's worth understanding what's happening to the leads you're already getting. In a lot of cases, fixing that is faster and cheaper than buying more traffic.
