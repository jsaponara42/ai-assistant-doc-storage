## Draft

There's a specific problem that happens when you build with AI in a room full of people.

You sit down to solve one problem. You start building. Something functional appears on the screen in twenty minutes. And then someone says "oh, could it also do this?"

And because it looks so easy, the answer feels like it should be yes.

That's where the MVP dies.

Vibe coding makes building feel cheap. And when building feels cheap, adding features feels cheap too. So you add one lane. Then another. Then someone from a different team sees it and has three more ideas. The original problem, the one you actually sat down to solve, starts to fade. You're now building something that does everything adequately instead of something that does one thing well.

This isn't a new problem. Scope creep is as old as projects. But AI accelerates it because the barrier to "let's just try it" is basically gone. You're not asking an engineer to spend two weeks on a feature. You're asking a model to take a shot at it right now.

The discipline required to ship an MVP with AI is higher than it used to be, not lower. You have to be more decisive about what's out of scope, not less. You have to be willing to say "not yet" when everything feels possible.

The tool is fast. The thinking still has to be slow.
