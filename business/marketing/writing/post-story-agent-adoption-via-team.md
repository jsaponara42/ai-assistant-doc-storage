## Draft

I've been building AI tools at Maslansky + Partners for a while now. And I learned something about adoption the hard way.

When I'm the one presenting a new AI skill or agent to the team, it lands okay. People are polite. Some try it. Most go back to what they were doing.

When a staffer on the team delivers the same thing to their peers, something different happens. People use it.

Same tool. Same explanation. Completely different result.

I've thought about why this is. Part of it is trust. When someone you work alongside every day tells you something saves them time, you believe them in a way you don't believe the person whose job it is to say that. There's no agenda to account for. They're not trying to justify a project or hit a metric. They just found something useful and they're sharing it.

Part of it is also proximity. A peer can show you exactly how it fits into the work you both do. I can explain the concept. They can show the specific moment in a specific workflow where it actually helps.

The lesson I took from this is that AI adoption inside an organization is not a top-down problem. You can have the best tools, the clearest explanation, the most patient rollout. If the path to the team runs only through the person responsible for AI, you've already lost half the room.

Find the people who get it early. Give them the tools and get out of the way.
