## Draft

Most businesses are building workflows that AI can't touch.

Not because the technology doesn't exist. Because their tools don't talk to each other.

You have a CRM that exports to CSV. A spreadsheet that lives on a shared drive. A form that emails one person who forwards it to someone else. None of those outputs are machine-readable. They require a human to open them, read them, and re-enter the data somewhere else.

That human bottleneck is what slows everything down.

Machine readability means your tools can exchange data without a middle person. APIs that pass structured data between systems. Command-line interfaces that let scripts do the work. Proper file formats that any tool can parse without guessing.

This isn't about AI replacing people. It's about removing the steps where people act as glue between tools that should already be connected.

The companies that move fast aren't the ones with the fanciest AI. They're the ones whose systems can actually talk to each other. Their data flows. Their tools integrate. Nobody sits between the output of one system and the input of the next.

If you're evaluating AI tools right now, the first question shouldn't be "what can it do?" It should be "can it actually connect to what I already have?"

Because a tool that requires copy-pasting data out of your existing systems is just another step in the friction chain. It solves one problem and creates three new ones.

Make your data machine-readable. Build your tools to exchange information directly. That's the infrastructure that makes AI actually useful.
