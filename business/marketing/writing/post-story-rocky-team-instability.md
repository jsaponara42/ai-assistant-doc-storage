## Draft

One of the things AI and automation make possible is moving fast at the start of a project. You can go from idea to something functional surprisingly quickly. That speed is real and it's genuinely useful.

What it also does is expose how much automation depends on things staying consistent.

We had a project — I'll call it Rocky — where we built out some solid early infrastructure. The automation logic was sound. The tools were right. And then the team changed. People shifted roles. Priorities moved. The people who understood why certain pieces were built the way they were weren't the ones running them anymore.

That's when things started to break.

Not the automation itself. The inputs. The handoffs. The assumptions baked into the process that made sense when the team built them and stopped making sense when the team changed.

This is the part of automation projects nobody talks about enough. The technology is usually the easy part. The hard part is that automation is only as stable as the process feeding it. And processes are run by people. When the people change, the process changes with them, whether anyone intended it to or not.

If you're building automation into a team that's still finding its footing, build for change. Document every assumption. Make the handoffs explicit. The automation will hold. Make sure the team around it can too.
