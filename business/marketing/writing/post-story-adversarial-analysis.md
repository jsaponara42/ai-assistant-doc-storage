## Draft

AI has a default setting: it agrees with you.

You share an idea, it tells you it's interesting. You share a plan, it helps you execute it. You share a piece of writing, it finds things to like. This is mostly useful and occasionally dangerous, because some of your ideas aren't good and you need something to tell you that before you act on them.

So I built a prompt that does the opposite.

I call it an adversarial analysis. I feed it an idea, a product concept, a piece of marketing, a business decision — anything I'm about to commit to — and it attacks it. Not randomly. Systematically. It goes after every assumption I've made, every claim that isn't proven, every place where the logic has a gap. It treats the thing I've created as if it's trying to find every reason it fails.

Then it tells me how to make it better.

The output is uncomfortable in a useful way. You find out what you didn't think about. You find the weak spots before someone else does. And the thing you end up with is stronger because it survived something designed to break it.

AI defaults to building you up. Sometimes you need a tool that tears it down first.
