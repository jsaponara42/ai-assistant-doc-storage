## Draft

At some point in a project, a client found a way to be profitable without the automation we were building.

Not a workaround. An actual viable path forward that was generating real cash flow. And the honest answer in that moment wasn't "let's keep building." It was "let's pause."

So we paused.

This is not the obvious move when you're an automation consultant. Pausing a project is pausing the engagement. But the alternative — continuing to build something for a process that hasn't been proven yet — is putting a client's time and money into infrastructure for something they don't know works.

The better play is straightforward. When a client finds a way to make money consistently, let them do that. Let them validate the model. When the cash flow is there and the process is proven, that's when automation starts to make sense. You're not automating a hypothesis anymore. You're automating something you know generates value.

And if you're the advisor who told them to pause when it was right to pause, you're the person they trust when it's time to start again.

The consultants who push projects forward for the sake of the engagement lose clients eventually. The ones who protect the client's interests keep them.

Being right for the business sometimes means being wrong for the invoice.
