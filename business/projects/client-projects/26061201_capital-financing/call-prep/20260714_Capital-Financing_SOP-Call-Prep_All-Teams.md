### Session Flow (shared shape, both days)

**1. Kickoff — 2 minutes**

Frame it simply: *"We're documenting how you actually do your job today — not how it should work, not the ideal version. That's what lets us find the real quick-win automations. There's no wrong answer here — if something's clunky or manual, say so, that's exactly what we want to hear."*

**2. Explain the dictation process — the most important part of the call**

Before anyone talks through their own workflow, explain the method itself:

- **What "dictating" means here:** talking through their role and how they currently handle a given workflow, out loud, in as much real detail as they'd naturally describe it to a new hire — not a polished or idealized version.
- **The six things to cover** (give this as a checklist people can keep in mind while they talk, not a form to fill out line by line):
  - What do you need to start this? (**Inputs**)
  - Where does that come from? (**Source**)
  - What software/tools do you touch? (**Systems**)
  - Who else is involved, and what's their part? (**Owners**)
  - What comes out the other end? (**Outputs**)
  - Where does that output go — who or what system receives it? (**Hand-off**)
- **Why it matters:** this raw, out-loud description is the actual source material — it's what eventually gets written up as a clean SOP and is also what surfaces the real automation opportunities, so the more honest and specific the dictation, the more useful the result.
- **No live demo from JC.** JC explains and facilitates but does not dictate his own workflow or run a Claude chat during this part — this is each attendee's own process to describe.

**3. Live dictation — each person talks through their own workflow**

Attendees dictate out loud, one at a time, in the priority order for that day (see day-specific sections below). JC facilitates:
- Prompt with the six-question checklist above if someone stalls or goes vague.
- Let people go in their own words first before redirecting — the gaps that surface naturally are usually the same gaps that make a process hard to hand off or automate.
- Don't rush past ambiguity — note it rather than smoothing it over; it becomes a `[TO CONFIRM]` later.

**4. Wrap-up — how dictations get turned into SOPs**

Not everything will get finished live — that's expected.
- Whatever is captured (notes taken live, or the attendee's own recollection after the call) gets written up afterward — either by the attendee running it through Claude themselves (see step 5) or sent to JC to process.
- Completed write-ups get emailed to JC at **`[TO CONFIRM — JC's preferred email/address for this]`** — plain copy-paste into the email body is fine, no special formatting needed.
- No filename convention required on their end — JC will fold it into the vault.

**5. Claude — shown at the very end**

This is the only point where Claude comes up. Show the group the prompt they (or JC, afterward) can paste into a Claude chat to turn a dictation into a clean written SOP:

> I'm going to describe my role and how I currently handle **[workflow name]** at Capital Financing. Ask me clarifying questions one at a time as I go if anything is unclear. When I say I'm done, write up everything I've described as a clean, numbered, step-by-step SOP that someone with no context could follow. Flag anything ambiguous or unconfirmed as `[TO CONFIRM]` rather than guessing.

Keep this brief — the point is showing them *that* this exists and how to use it, not running a live example.

**6. Claude Team setup — last, ~5 minutes, do not troubleshoot live**

Share the join link and have everyone click through and log in during the call:

```
https://claude.ai/join/org/Ccg9eZuKOphA-dMY23rWKw
```

Confirm each person can see they're in the Capital Financing workspace — that's the only success check needed live. **If anything doesn't work (login issue, wrong email, etc.), don't stop to fix it in the group** — tell them to message JC directly afterward and it'll get sorted one-on-one.

**7. What happens next**

Let the group know: once SOPs start coming in, JC may follow up individually if something needs more detail — a short one-on-one follow-up call is likely for at least one or two people, not a failure state, just normal next-pass clarification (same pattern as the `[TO CONFIRM]` items already in the existing workflow map).

### Thursday — Ops Team (Christy, Yasmine, Danielle)

**2. Yasmine — Contracting (Agreement Issuance & Execution)**

Why second: picks up right where Christy's output ends (approved terms → agreement sent → executed), and this step has several open `[TO CONFIRM]` gaps in the existing workflow map — e-sign platform, exact system used, what triggers agreement issuance.

### Friday — BDR Team (Audrey, Brian, Victoria)

**Why this order:** A more uneven group than Thursday's — one strong performer with a mature (if undocumented) process, one early-stage hire still forming habits, and one with known compliance and follow-up issues. Leading with the strongest, clearest process sets the tone and gives Claude (and JC) a working baseline to compare the other two against, rather than starting from the roughest example.

**1. Audrey — Case-Expense Onboarding Calls + her Lead-Handling Workflow — lead with this**

Why first: she's the strongest performer on the team ($1M+ generated, 3.5 years tenure) and — more importantly for this session — she's the specific consultant referenced in the workflow map's most costly documented breakpoint: an undocumented, ad-hoc onboarding approach that didn't transfer to a law firm contact, leading to a 20-in/20-out rework loop. There is currently **no standard onboarding script at all** — whatever gets captured from her today is likely to become the first draft of one.

What to get her talking through:

- Her actual lead follow-up cadence and habits — the "why does my follow-up work when others' doesn't" version, not just the steps
- If time allows: how she currently handles the boundary between her side business and Capital Financing leads, since that's a live guardrail conversation with Howie right now

**2. Brian — Lead Handling, with a focus on Case-Expense vs. Pre-Settlement split**

Why second: he's new (about a month in) and the CEO has already flagged a specific imbalance — he's over-indexing on case-expense leads relative to the target 1:7 ratio with pre-settlement. Documenting his actual current process (not the target one) is useful diagnostic input in its own right, separate from Audrey's more mature process.

What to get him talking through:
- How a lead reaches him and what he does with it end to end
- Where in that process he's deciding case-expense vs. pre-settlement focus
- What happens after he passes an opportunity back to Howie or a teammate — does he track whether it gets executed

**3. Victoria — Lead Handling / Follow-up + Conference Lead Handling**

Why last: lower priority to lead with given known compliance and follow-up gaps, but still worth capturing — partly because her actual working process (however thin) is still useful raw material, and partly because a performance review/possible exit is already in motion per the CEO's notes, which makes capturing what she does know now more time-sensitive, not less.

What to get her talking through:
- What she actually does when a lead or conference contact is assigned to her — walk the real process, not the ideal one
- Where geography (South Florida-only pattern) shows up as a practical constraint in how she works a list
- Keep this one light-touch and non-judgmental — the goal is documentation, not a performance conversation; that stays separate and with Howie

**Shared thread across all three (Friday):** All three touch Workflow 5 Step 5 (post-conference list assignment and follow-up) — the single most-cited failure point in the whole account (~$20K/event spend, low conversion). If time allows after the three individual walkthroughs, a quick round on "what do you actually do with a conference list once it's handed to you" from each person would be high-value, even briefly.
