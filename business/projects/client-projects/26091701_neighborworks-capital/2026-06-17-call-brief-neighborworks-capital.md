# Call Brief — NeighborWorks Capital
**Date:** ___________
**Industry:** CDFI / community development finance (nonprofit lender)

> *Industry primer (read first):* A **CDFI (Community Development Financial Institution)** is a federally certified lender whose mission is to put financing into low-income or underserved communities that mainstream banks tend to skip — here, that means funding affordable housing. Think of NeighborWorks Capital as a mission-driven bank: it borrows money cheaply from big banks, foundations, and the government, then re-lends it to local nonprofit housing developers on patient, flexible terms. It makes its money on the interest spread, and answers to the funders who supply its capital.

---

## The Company
- **What they do:** National nonprofit CDFI providing flexible, patient loans + consulting to the ~250-member NeighborWorks America network. ("Patient" loans = longer timelines and more forgiving terms than a commercial bank would offer; the NeighborWorks America network is a nationwide group of local nonprofit housing organizations, and NeighborWorks Capital is the lending arm that serves them.) More than $600 million deployed to date.
- **Size:** ~24–26 staff (16–50 employee tier). Headquarters in Silver Spring, Maryland (MD).
- **Stage:** Established (founded 2000) but in an active growth + modernization phase — heavy 2024–2026 senior hiring.
- **Tech signals:** Salesforce (a customer-relationship-management platform — the central database tracking borrowers and loans; they run a borrower portal on it and are actively expanding it), a WordPress website, and a dedicated technology-operations role. Mid-maturity: not paper-based, but still actively building their systems.
- **Notable:** Rated 4 stars by **AERIS** (an independent rating agency that scores CDFIs on financial health and mission impact, similar to a credit rating — a strong signal to their funders). New Chief Executive Officer (CEO) in 2022, plus four new senior executives ("C-suite") since 2023. A dedicated Salesforce-expansion project is underway (led by staffer Brendan Barry). Manual portfolio reporting is a named job function — a hint that pulling loan data together is still hands-on work.

## Anticipated Pains
1. **Manual portfolio & investor reporting.** "Portfolio" = the full set of loans NWC currently has outstanding. They must regularly report on it by hand to: their **loan committee** (the internal group that approves loans), their board, their investors (the banks/foundations who funded them and want to see how the money is performing), and the **CDFI Fund** (the U.S. Treasury program that certifies CDFIs and gives them grants — it requires compliance reporting in exchange). Assembling all this by hand is recurring, deadline-driven, and error-prone.
2. **Borrower data collection bottleneck.** Chasing borrowers (the local housing developers who took the loans) for up-to-date financial statements through the portal. Follow-up is manual, and stale data stalls reporting and loan monitoring.
3. **Loan closing & draw-processing admin.** "Closing" = the document-heavy final step where a loan is signed and funded. A "draw" is when a borrower requests a chunk of an already-approved construction or pre-development loan as the project hits milestones — each request needs review and paperwork. Both are handled largely by hand across a small team. ("Pre-development" = the early, risky planning phase of a housing project, before construction starts.)
4. **Salesforce expansion outpacing capacity.** They're actively expanding their Salesforce system but with essentially one project manager on it — so the integration, automation, and staff adoption likely lag behind the ambition.
5. **Compliance & documentation burden.** CDFI Fund requirements, the AERIS rating, and investor **covenants** (contractual promises to funders — e.g. "we'll keep losses below X%" — that must be tracked and proven) all demand structured, repeatable documentation. That's a heavy lift for a ~25-person shop.

## Sharp Questions to Ask
- "Walk me through how a portfolio or investor report comes together today — who touches it, and how many hours does one cycle take?"
- "When a borrower's financials are due, how does that follow-up actually happen — and how often does missing data hold up your reporting?"
- "Where are you in the Salesforce expansion, and what's the gap between what you want it to do and what it does today?"
- "What's taking the most time in your operation right now that you wish wasn't?"
- "What have you already tried to fix the manual reporting side, and where did it fall short?"
- "Rachel — what eats the most of your day that feels like it shouldn't be a person's job?"

---

## What to Listen For
Tiana naming a specific recurring report or data-pull that burns real hours, or describing the Salesforce build as under-resourced, is a strong fit. If she frames the problem as "we know what we want, we just can't get there with current capacity," that's a ready-to-move signal. A concrete, time-quantified pain (not a vague "we could be more efficient") means move to a discovery session — a deeper follow-up call where you scope the actual work.

## Red Flags
Neither attendee is C-suite, so budget authority isn't in the room — confirm who signs off before scoping anything paid. Watch for "we just hired people / bought Salesforce to handle this" — they may feel the problem is already being solved internally. As a CDFI funded by grants + investor capital (rather than profits it can freely spend), procurement can be slow and cautious; expect a longer path to a signed deal than with a for-profit company of the same size.
