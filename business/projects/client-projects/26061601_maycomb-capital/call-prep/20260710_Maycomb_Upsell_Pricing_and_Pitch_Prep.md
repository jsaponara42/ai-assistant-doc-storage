Four upsell tracks, priced against Blue Tusk's existing pricing guide and the Cause Crazy comp, with a pitch built from Maycomb's own words rather than a generic sales case. Ranked by likelihood to land given who's actually in the room right now.

## The Four Tracks

### Track D — AI / Claude Steward (Retainer)
**What it is:** Ongoing oversight of the Claude rollout, monitoring the Slack gripe channel, building training resources as new hires onboard, curating the SKILLS library, attending the existing bi-weekly Roadmap Meeting for a monthly review.

**Price:** $750–$1,200/month. Blue Tusk's standard advisory rate ($2,500/month) is calibrated for 50–300 person orgs; a 12-person team generates a fraction of that usage volume and gripe-channel traffic, so this is priced down accordingly. Month-to-month, cancel anytime.

**The undeniable hook:** This isn't a pitch, it's a direct answer to a problem the client doc already named in their own words: *"Nobody owns AI adoption."* That line is already sitting in a document Martina has read. The ask is simply "we said this was a gap, here's how we close it."

**Why it lands:** Low dollar amount. Recurring, cancel-anytime framing lowers the bar for a yes. Directly serves Martina's own stated values (systems, structure, accountability) from her *Scale with Purpose* background. Doesn't require Andi's sign-off for most orgs this size, worth confirming, but a sub-$1,500/month recurring vendor cost is unlikely to need Managing Partner approval the way a five-figure project would.

### Track A — Quick Wins Implementation
**What it is:** Build the three automations already scoped and impact-estimated in the roadmap: eliminate duplicate invoice tracking with LeverPoint, automate AP expense categorization, auto-draft quarterly borrower invoices.

**Price:** $4,500–$6,500 fixed fee for the bundle, or $1,500–$2,000 per automation à la carte. Cause Crazy paid $3,500 total for a comparable bundle of three no-code automations; this sits higher because Maycomb's builds touch financial data, need LLM-assisted logic (not pure Zapier triggers), and require LeverPoint coordination on at least two of the three.

**Payment structure:** 50% upfront, 50% on delivery, mirroring Blue Tusk's standard deposit structure.

**The undeniable hook:** Every one of these is tied to a departure. The shared mailbox eases Varenka's handoff. The categorization automation removes a dependency on Danielle's memory, and she's already half-gone. The invoicing automation removes the most time-consuming manual step in a process about to lose its owner. None of this is hypothetical ROI, it's "the person who does this by hand is leaving in weeks."

**Why it lands:** Bounded scope, fixed price, tangible before/after. Easier to say yes to a $5K fixed project than an open-ended retainer. Best pitched as urgent given the transition timeline, not as a nice-to-have.

**Discretionary option:** The SOW allows Blue Tusk to build one small quick win for free, at its discretion, as a no-charge goodwill gesture. If you want a free trust-builder before asking for money on anything else, this is the lever, offer the smallest one (mailbox) free, price the other two.

### Track C — Value Driver Discovery (Phase 3)
**What it is:** The deep-dive discovery Ariella's own conversation opened up, deal sourcing and diligence, investor relations and capital raising, borrower asset management, outcome data and impact reporting, mapped the same way the back office was mapped, but this time as a paid engagement.

**Price:** $2,500–$3,000/month for an initial 2–3 month discovery phase, converting into scoped build SOWs once specific opportunities are validated. Not a fixed fee, the scope genuinely isn't known yet, and pricing it as fixed would be dishonest.

**The undeniable hook:** Ariella said it herself, on the record: the impact report is *"such a difficult process"* that *"doesn't need to be"* that hard. That's not Blue Tusk's read on the business, it's the client's own words about their own pain, already captured in the discovery brief. Pair it with the new Investor Relations hire starting August 3, who inherits this process cold and would benefit from having it already mapped before he arrives.

**Why it's a slower close:** This is the highest long-term value track and the least defined. It touches revenue-critical processes the deal team owns, not just Martina's operational scope, and it's priced as a retainer with unknown total cost, which is a harder ask mid-transition. Best pitched as "let's put this on the calendar," not closed today.

### Track B — Strategic Investments
**What it is:** The four larger back-office builds: legal bill allocation, full cross-entity AP allocation, the budget variance flagging and recommendation engine, and outcome-data intake for the annual impact report.

**Price:** $10,000–$15,000 total, structured as a 2–3 month project at $3,500–$5,000/month, or priced per-build if Maycomb wants to pick and choose ($3,000–$5,000 each, budget variance engine at the top of that range given its complexity).

**The undeniable hook:** Ariella named the exact outcome she wanted, unprompted: compress the quarterly budget review to *"a single approval meeting."* That's about as close to a client writing your pitch for you as it gets.

**Why it's the slowest close right now:** This is the biggest dollar ask, requires sustained LeverPoint coordination, and lands exactly when nobody wants to make a five-figure financial commitment mid-transition, with no permanent Director of Finance & Ops yet in place to own it. Andi's approval is close to certain to be required here. **Recommend holding this track until the Director of Finance & Ops is hired**, then pitching it directly to them as the new process owner, rather than asking Martina to champion someone else's future budget line.

### Track E — Portfolio Company Referral Pipeline (not Maycomb revenue)

**What it is:** Ariella raised this herself, unprompted, on the original June 16, 2026 kickoff call: since Danielle's SOP work was already happening as she prepared to leave, and some portfolio companies likely have similar operational pain, Ariella floated introducing Blue Tusk to portfolio companies that are struggling. Her exact words left it deliberately open, not committed: *"we can have more conversations about whether or not it makes sense to be introduced to portfolio companies and how that might work."*

**Why this isn't like the other four tracks:** No revenue from Maycomb here. Any paid work would be a new, separate engagement directly with the portfolio company. This is a referral channel, not an upsell.

**What's changed since she said it:** Ariella's gone. Martina, the current primary contact, wasn't in that room and has no reason to know this was ever discussed. Treat it as needing to be reintroduced, not as something still live.

**Two conditions Ariella put on it herself, worth respecting:**
1. Scoped to companies that are *struggling*, not a blanket intro across the whole portfolio.
2. Maycomb cares openly about confidentiality (came up on the same call, re: the MSA). Any introduction needs Maycomb's active blessing per company, not a standing open door Blue Tusk works independently.

**The smooth way in:** Don't pitch this as its own ask in the upsell conversation, it'll read as padding. It surfaces naturally as a byproduct of Track C, specifically VD-2 (borrower asset management). If that discovery work maps which portfolio companies are struggling operationally, that's the exact moment Ariella herself described as the right one for an introduction. Let the work create the opening rather than asking for it upfront.

**If it's worth a light mention sooner:** One low-pressure line is enough, something like: *"Early on, Ariella mentioned that if we ever came across portfolio companies that seemed to be struggling with similar things, an introduction might make sense. Just flagging that's still on the table whenever it's useful, no rush."* That reopens the door without asking for anything now.

## How to Open the Conversation

Don't build a new pitch deck. The client doc already ends on this exact bridge, the "What's Next" section names four problems and gestures at solutions. Walk Martina and Barry through that section live, then get specific with pricing only once they're nodding at the problems. The document did the persuading already; this conversation is just putting numbers on what they already agreed was missing.

Suggested order: open with Track D (cheapest, most obviously needed), confirm it, then move to Track A (tangible, urgent), then mention Track C as "the bigger opportunity, let's revisit once you're settled in." Leave Track B unsaid unless they ask.
