# Call Brief — Maycomb Capital
**Date:** 2026-06-16
**Industry:** Impact investing / private credit (fund manager)

> **Quick orientation for an industry outsider:** Maycomb is a *fund manager* — a company that pools money from outside investors and lends or invests it on their behalf, aiming to earn a return. What makes them unusual is the "impact" angle: they specifically fund programs that help underserved communities (early childhood education, workforce training, affordable housing), and they measure social results, not just financial ones. "Private credit" means they make loans privately (outside of banks and public bond markets) to organizations that need capital.

---

## The Company
- **What they do:** Impact investing platform providing mission-aligned debt and equity to social enterprises.
  - *Debt* = loans the borrower repays with interest. *Equity* = buying an ownership stake in a company. *Social enterprise* = an organization (for-profit or non-profit) whose core purpose is solving a social problem.
  - Their flagship is the **Community Outcomes Fund** — the largest US fund dedicated to *outcomes financing*, also called *pay-for-success*. (Explained in "Anticipated Pains" below — it's the core mechanic of their business, so it's worth understanding well.)
  - They also run a *custom strategies* arm (tailor-made investment approaches built for a specific large investor — e.g., a foundation that wants its money used a particular way) and **The Social Entrepreneurs Fund (TSEF)**, which buys early-stage ownership stakes in promising mission-driven companies.
- **Size:** ~16–20 person team, Brooklyn headquarters (with a presence in Denver). Roughly **$140M** (140 million dollars) in **AUM (Assets Under Management** — the total amount of investor money the firm is responsible for investing) as of 2023.
- **Stage:** Established and growing — founded 2016, actively hiring (an Investment Associate and an Investment Analyst), and launching new investment vehicles such as the Outcomes Finance Accelerator Fund, run jointly with the ZOMA Foundation. (A "vehicle" is just industry shorthand for a specific fund or legal structure that holds and deploys money.)
- **Tech signals:** Website built on Gatsby with **DatoCMS** (the software running their website's content — not relevant to operations, just an observation about their tech maturity). They have a web contact form and newsletter signup, but **no visible CRM (Customer Relationship Management** system — software like Salesforce for tracking contacts, leads, and relationships), no deal-flow software (a tool for tracking potential investments as they move through review), and no visible fund-administration tooling. Their newest fund invites applications by "email, video, voice memo, or a napkin" — a strong sign their intake of new opportunities is manual and unstructured.
- **Notable:** Women-owned. Named to the **ImpactAssets IA50** (an annual list of 50 noteworthy impact-investing fund managers — a credibility marker in their world). They just held their first **AGM (Annual General Meeting** — a yearly gathering where a fund updates its investors), with 60+ investors attending. Their model carries a heavy reporting burden because, as explained below, they only get paid when measurable social results are proven.

## Anticipated Pains

> **Core concept first — how their main business works:** In **outcomes financing** (a.k.a. **pay-for-success**), a government agrees to pay *only if* a social program actually delivers agreed-upon results — for example, "we'll pay if X% of participants find jobs and keep them." But the program needs money up front to operate. Maycomb provides that up-front money as a loan, the program runs, results are measured, and if the targets are hit, the government repays Maycomb. The whole model lives or dies on *measuring and proving outcomes* — which is exactly why so much of their pain is about data and reporting.

1. **Outcomes / impact reporting is manual.** Every pay-for-success deal depends on collecting, verifying, and reporting outcome data from the service providers running the programs and the governments paying for results — work that's likely done in spreadsheets and email, one deal at a time.
2. **Investor / LP reporting and communications.** They just held their first AGM with 60+ investors. An **LP (Limited Partner** — the outside investors who put money *into* the fund; the firm itself is the "General Partner" that manages it) expects regular updates. Quarterly LP updates, **capital calls** (formal requests for investors to send in money they've already committed), and custom impact write-ups are time-intensive and recur constantly for a 5-person finance team.
3. **Unstructured deal intake.** New-fund applications arrive by "email, video, voice memo, or napkin." With no structured pipeline, every incoming opportunity requires manual sorting, data entry, and inconsistent capture — easy for things to slip through.
4. **Loan monitoring and portfolio administration.** Servicing private loans means ongoing tracking of **covenants** (conditions written into a loan that the borrower must keep meeting — e.g., maintaining a certain cash level), borrower financial statements, and disbursement schedules (the timed release of loan money) across many for-profit and non-profit borrowers. This recurring back-office load grows with every new deal. (*"Portfolio"* = the full collection of loans/investments the fund currently holds.)
5. **Knowledge / diligence synthesis.** Judging "what works" means reading long program evaluations, government contracts, and research evidence. (*"Diligence,"* short for *due diligence*, is the investigation a firm does before committing money, to confirm an opportunity is sound.) This is research-heavy and well-suited to AI summarization.

## Sharp Questions to Ask
- "Walk me through what happens from a new outcomes deal coming in to the first investor report going out — where does your team spend the most hours?"
- "How are you collecting and validating outcome data from your service providers and government partners today?"
- "After the AGM — how much of the quarterly investor and impact reporting is hand-built each cycle?"
- "With the new Accelerator Fund taking applications in any format — how are you triaging and organizing that inbound right now?"
- "What's taking the most time in your operation right now that you wish wasn't?"
- "As you add headcount and deals, what breaks first in your current setup?"

## What to Listen For
A strong fit sounds like Ariella naming a specific recurring workflow that eats her small team's time — outcome data wrangling, investor reporting, or intake triage — and Danielle nodding because she's the one doing it. Listen for "we do this every quarter by hand," "it doesn't scale," or anxiety tied to the new fund and new hires. Both the decision-maker (Ariella, who holds the budget) and the implementer (Danielle, who'd use the tool) being on the call is itself a green light to move toward a discovery session (the next, deeper meeting where you'd map their workflows in detail).

---

## Red Flags
Mission-driven, lean teams can treat manual process as a virtue (the "napkin" framing) rather than a cost — watch for resistance to automating work that feels high-touch or relationship-based. Data sensitivity (government, beneficiary, and financial information) may raise legitimate caution around AI governance — i.e., their internal rules for what AI tools are allowed to touch sensitive data. And as a regulated fund manager (a firm subject to financial-industry rules and oversight), anything touching investment decisions or compliance will move slowly — so anchor the conversation on back-office time savings, not on the firm's investment judgment.
