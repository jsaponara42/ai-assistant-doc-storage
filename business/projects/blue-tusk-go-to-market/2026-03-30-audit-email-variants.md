## Email 3 · Day 3

**What's being tested:** probing process inconsistency (A) vs. leading with a dollar figure (B)

---

### Variant A — Process inconsistency
*Asks a question they know the uncomfortable answer to. Ties the problem directly to automation.*

**Subject Line:** quick question about your team

**Preview Text:** {{firstName}}, is your intake process the same whether it's your best staff member or your newest hire?

---

{{firstName}},

Is your intake process documented well enough that a new hire would run it the same way your most experienced person does?

For most PI firms the answer is no. The process lives in people's heads, which means it changes depending on who's working, what else is happening, and how busy the week is.

Simplifying the workflow and automating away the inconsistency. That's what the audit's for.

Worth chatting about?

,John-Carlos

---

### Variant B — Dollar figure lead
*Opens with a number that's hard to ignore, then ties it to automation. More direct ask at the end.*

---

{{firstName}},

PI firms typically spend 20ish hours a week on intake follow-up, status calls, document requests, and onboarding paperwork. That's $20-70k a year in waste. Not even counting what you could be doing with all that time instead.

It's a tech problem and it's a workflow problem. The tasks need to be done. They just don't need a human to do them - and that's exactly what we build automation for.

Our audit finds exactly which tasks those are in your firm and what fixing them is worth. Free, one call. I think you might be a good fit.

Curious if the number feels close to what you're seeing. Worth a quick call over the next couple weeks?

,John-Carlos

They know the systems need work, but they'll get to it when things calm down a bit.

Things don't calm down. The caseload fills the time available for it, and the system problems get another 12 months of being normalized.

If the timing is ever right, I'm around. Just shoot me a "hello".

,John-Carlos

## Testing Notes

**Email 1:** A vs B tests whether curiosity or the offer itself is the stronger hook. Variant A ends with a personality line ("if I creep you out") that could disarm or polarize — worth watching reply sentiment. If B wins, the qualifying frame ("firms that qualify") is doing work worth leaning into.

**Email 3:** A vs B tests process-gap discomfort vs. a dollar figure. If B wins, financial framing is more motivating than operational pain for this audience — useful signal for future sequences.

**Email 4:** A vs B tests two different objections. A addresses "I'll get to it later." B addresses "I just need to hire someone." Whichever performs better tells you which belief is the bigger barrier to booking a call.

**Email 5:** A vs B tests an open exit vs. a low-friction yes/no close. B will likely produce more replies. Track whether those replies convert to calls at the same rate as A before declaring a winner.

**Sequence compatibility:** every variant is written to stand alone. None reference specific things said in previous emails, so any combination works without contradiction.
