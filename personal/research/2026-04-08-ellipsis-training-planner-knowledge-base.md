## Summary

Distilled skill and technique reference from Chase Hughes' *Ellipsis Training Planner* (the 52-week workbook companion to The Ellipsis Manual). Organized by skill domain, not by week. Source is the workbook — not following the weekly schedule, using this purely as a knowledge reference.

---

## The Ellipsis Progression

The full sequence of a behavior engineering interaction, which can be compressed to as little as 30 seconds or extended across a long conversation:

1. **Awareness development** — observe behavior, establish baseline
2. **Initial control (FIC)** — establish Focus, Interest, Curiosity in the subject
3. **Rapport** — build connection through mirroring, GHT, sensory matching
4. **Authority** — the operator's credibility and presence; the foundation of everything
5. **Confusion / autopilot shutoff** — break the subject's habitual mental state
6. **Trance induction** — dissociation, regression, sleep deprivation methods
7. **Activation** — embedded commands, calls to action, scarcity/regret
8. **Anchoring / entrainment** — reinforce and condition behavioral compliance

> Note: This is a full-sequence map. Most real interactions use a truncated version. Confusion (step 5) is the minimum entry point; everything before it makes it more effective.

## Skill Domains

### 1. Behavior observation

The foundational skill. All influence depends on being able to read behavior without burning conscious attention on it — i.e. moving observation to unconscious competence.

**Key behaviors to observe:**
- **Body/posture:** Spinal angle (lean toward = interest, away = disinterest), postural shifts in response to topics
- **Hands:** Direction of movement toward or away from torso; gesture hemisphere (GHT)
- **Feet/lower body:** The farther from the head, the harder to consciously control — lower body is often the most honest signal. Watch for foot retreat during conversation.
- **Face:** The 7 basic facial expressions; microexpressions (rare, not a deception signal on their own); shutter speed (eyelid blink rate)
- **Eyes:** Blink rate, pupil dilation/constriction, gaze direction on memory recall (consistent per individual = their pattern), eye contact duration
- **Breathing:** Chest vs. abdomen breathing; rate shifts during conversation; use clothing movement and speech patterns to observe
- **Voice:** Pitch rises with emotional state; changes in volume, speed, clarity

**The 4 behavior types (BToE categories):**
- **Open** — receptive, engaged; these are entry points for influence
- **Closed** — defensive, withdrawn; hitting a nerve or wrong topic; change course
- **Unsure** — insecure, self-focused; can signal vulnerability or resistance
- **Variable** — behaviors that shift; barometers of in-the-moment mood change

**Deception signals:** Scored using the DRS (Deception Rating Scale) on the BToE. No single behavior = deception. Look for a cluster of 4.0-rated behaviors and total DRS score above 10. Nervousness and deception produce similar signals — context is the differentiator.

### 2. Needs mapping and profiling

**The Needs Map:** A working list of core human needs used as influence levers. Needs are usually desires to *feel* a certain way in order to *avoid* discomfort.

**How to identify needs:** Listen to what people talk about most, what they seek validation on, what compliments stick for them. Ask what they like most about their job, their proudest moments, what others compliment them on.

**Fear is the inverse of need.** Once a need is identified, the associated fear becomes a usable lever (in sales: creating urgency; in rapport: addressing the vulnerability directly).

**The Hughes Social Stability Scale:** A quick profiling tool measuring social stability, suggestibility, and self-esteem. Output format: `[Need] [Need] [Score1, Score2, Score3]` e.g. "Appreciation Intelligence 2,1,1." Not diagnostic — an estimation tool.

**Suffering first:** Before profiling for influence, recognize the person's suffering. Everyone hides it in some form. Seeing it humanizes the subject and creates genuine empathy that supports rapport.

---

### 3. Authority

The single most important variable. Authority without influence skills outperforms influence skills without authority.

**What authority is:** Not dominance or aggression. Calm, controlled, relaxed confidence. Genuine interest and concern (like a doctor or coach). Comfortable issuing directions without needing permission.

**How to develop it:**
- Issue commands in place of questions ("Hand me that" vs "Could you pass that?") — politely, not rudely
- Start conversations with strangers proactively
- Monitor internal dialogue during conversations — notice when it's anxious or self-focused
- Shift demeanor toward calm, relaxed, other-focused presence
- Adjust appearance to trigger agentic responses (wardrobe, hygiene, posture, eye contact)
- Stop waiting for permission to take confident action

**Key insight from the Obedience Experiment (Milgram):** A man with zero influence training convinced strangers to commit simulated murder using authority alone. Authority is the multiplier for all other skills.

---

### 4. Rapport

**Mirroring:** Replicate sequences of behavior (not individual actions) — posture, vocal characteristics, hand motion, breathing rhythm. Mirror for ~60 seconds, then lead with a deliberate change and observe if they follow. Following = rapport confirmed.

**GHT (Gestural Hemispheric Tendency):** People gesture toward the hemisphere associated with positive memories. Identify which side by asking about happy experiences and watching where they gesture. Then step to that side and gesture from it when speaking — subconsciously aligns you with positive associations.

**Sensory preference:** People default to visual, auditory, or kinesthetic language. Identify their dominant channel from their word choices. Mirror their sensory language back in conversation — creates deep unconscious resonance.

**Breathing matching:** Exhale as the subject speaks. Shift breathing toward their pattern. One of the hardest skills to execute in real-time — needs other mechanics solid first.

**Mini confessions:** Deliberate small admissions of imperfection during conversation — builds trust and lowers the subject's guard.

**Cold reading:** Positive cold reading specifically — point out hidden strengths and genuine traits you observe. Makes the subject more open, less critical of themselves and the interaction. Opens doors. Starter phrases: "I get a sense that you're the kind of person who…", "Just from knowing you a few minutes, I can tell you…"

---

### 5. FIC — Focus, Interest, Curiosity

The required state in the subject before any deeper influence work can land. Establishing all three is the prerequisite to trance induction, embedded commands, and activation.

**How to develop it:** Use the word "focus" in conversation with emphasis. Use gestural markers ([op] pointing to your own chest or face, or the hallway gesture) when delivering focus-related language. Speak with genuine conviction about whatever is being discussed. Mirror the level of excitement you want to see.

---

### 6. Confusion (autopilot shutoff)

The primary tool for breaking the subject's habitual mental state and creating a receptive window.

**How it works:** Confusion creates temporary cognitive disarray — the subject's critical thinking is briefly impaired, and they instinctively seek resolution. That resolution window is when suggestions and commands land most cleanly. Described as: the subject is like a drowning person grasping for anything to stabilize on.

**Rules for constructing a confusion statement:**
- Must sound logically coherent even though it isn't
- Use double negatives
- Blend senses, time, and awareness
- Tone, volume, and cadence matter as much as content
- After delivering it, immediately offer clear direction to re-establish closure — this is what the subject grabs onto

**Delivery notes:** Start by using confusion statements on their own before adding commands. Build confident delivery first. Can be used over the phone. Best combined with authority.

**Pattern interrupts (physical):** Dropping keys, sudden movement, anything that breaks the flow of a subject's sentence mid-stream. The critical factor (castle guards) is at rest when they're talking — interrupting with something unusual creates the same receptive window.

---

### 7. Gestural markers

Deliberate gestures tied to specific words or phrases to covertly anchor associations in the subject's unconscious.

- **[op] gesture** — toward yourself (face/chest); used when mentioning traits you want the subject to associate with you, or when saying words like "focus"
- **[sp] gesture** — toward the subject; used when mentioning positive traits you want them to identify with themselves, or to activate trust

**Practice:** Make these flow naturally before deploying. They must appear conversational. Over time they become automatic.

---

### 8. Linguistics

**Third-party metaphor:** Tell a story about someone else while shifting pronouns toward the listener. The message bypasses conscious resistance because the subject doesn't register it as directed at them.

**Presuppositions:** Statements that assume the desired outcome is already happening or inevitable. Bypasses objection by treating it as foregone.

**Double binds:** Offer two choices that both lead to the desired outcome. Gives the subject a sense of autonomy while guiding them toward agreement. Easier to execute than embedded commands — good place to start.

**Embedded commands:** Commands nested inside sentences so the sentence sounds complete without them. Separated with pauses and slight vocal shift. e.g. "I was reading about how people just *[command]* when they feel understood." Must be pre-written and rehearsed until they feel conversational. Modify the command list every two weeks.

**Speed matching:** Match the subject's speech speed, then gradually slow yours. Watch for them to follow.

**Social validation:** Suggest the desired behavior is widely accepted. "Most people in your situation tend to…"

**Scarcity and regret:** Create urgency by emphasizing limited availability or the cost of inaction. Best used to close — not as a standalone opener. Watch for downward gaze as the subject processes it (processing signal).

---

### 9. Dissociation

Gradually separates the subject from their immediate sense of self — creating a third-person perspective. The subject becomes less resistant to choices that would normally raise flags.

**When to use:** When you anticipate needing the subject to make choices that might trigger their critical factor. Initiate early in the conversation if so.

**When NOT to use:** If you plan to use regression — the two don't combine well. Dissociation waters down regression by pulling the subject into third-person memory rather than first-person experience.

**Pronoun shifts:** Watch for subjects naturally shifting to "you" or "one" when discussing themselves — signal of emerging dissociation.

---

### 10. Regression

Guides the subject into a more open, less defended mental state by reawakening early sensory memories. Lowers inhibition and creates a window for acceptance and action.

**How to initiate:** Ask questions about childhood homes, holiday memories, specific sensory details of early experiences. The goal is vivid first-person recall, not analysis.

**Priming for regression:** Ask about a shared cultural memory from their childhood first (a product, a historical moment). Then transition into personal questions.

**Physical signals of successful regression:** Shoulders drop, muscles around the lower eye relax, posture opens. These are emotional changes made visible.

---

### 11. Scarcity, regret, and activation

**Scarcity:** Emphasize that time, opportunity, or access is limited.

**Regret narratives:** Prompt the subject to recall missed opportunities — creates motivation to act now.

**Activation phrases:** Move the subject toward a desired action using excitement (what they gain) or regret avoidance (what they lose). "Go first" — match the energy level you want to produce in them.

**Call to action:** Close the conversation with a specific directive. Use double-bind framing ("the question isn't whether you do this, it's whether you do it now or later"). Watch for action behaviors: increased posture, upper eyelid raise, nostril flaring, forward lean.

---

### 12. Anchoring

Associates a specific sensory trigger with a desired emotional state, allowing that state to be re-elicited later.

**Types of anchors:** Touch, visual cue, sound, olfactory. The "now" gesture (pointing to a wristwatch) is a standard activation anchor.

**Three steps:** (1) Elicit the desired emotional state through questions/stories; (2) Set the anchor at the peak of the state; (3) Test and fire the anchor later.

**Note:** Anchoring and dissociation don't combine — keep the subject in first-person (associated) during anchoring.

---

### 13. Entrainment

Repeated small compliance behaviors that build a pattern of following. Requires authority to be present or results are minimal.

**Key moves:** Request a small favor and thank them. Step back while speaking confidently — creates a vacuum the subject steps into. Point to an object during conversation and observe compliance. Pick your back up off a chair as you deliver a call to action.

---

### 14. Planning process (OIPL)

For pre-planned interactions:

- **Outcome:** Desired end state — emotions, thoughts about you, actions you want, post-interaction safety
- **Intelligence:** What you know about the subject beforehand — needs, fears, family, beliefs
- **Process:** The sequence of methods from first contact to call to action; what emotional states to create at each stage
- **Language:** Specific phrases pre-written and tailored to the subject's personality and sensory preference

## Operator principles (from the final page)

- See the brain, not the person
- See suffering first — humanize before profiling
- Authority is the foundation; influence skills rest on it
- Never let the subject see you as the cause of their decisions
- Go first — match the emotional/physical state you want to produce
- No permission needed

## Skill sequence for practice (full map)

Weeks 1–8 of the planner are all observation only — no influence attempts. This is intentional: observation must move to unconscious competence before any influence skill can execute cleanly. Trying to observe AND influence simultaneously burns all available conscious attention.

Rough progression order for solo development:
1. Behavior observation (all types)
2. Needs mapping + fear identification
3. Authority development (ongoing — never stops)
4. Rapport (mirroring, GHT, sensory match)
5. FIC development
6. Cold reading
7. Confusion / autopilot shutoff
8. Gestural markers
9. Linguistics (presuppositions, double binds, embedded commands)
10. Dissociation OR regression (not both together)
11. Scarcity / regret / activation
12. Anchoring
13. Entrainment
14. Full combination (planning + sequencing)
